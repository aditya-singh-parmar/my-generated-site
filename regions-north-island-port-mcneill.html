                      Port McNeill, Alert Bay and Sointula

                      <div class="gdlr-content"> <!-- Above Sidebar Section--> <div class="above-sidebar-wrapper"><section id="content-section-1"><div class="gdlr-full-size-wrapper gdlr-show-all" style="padding-bottom: 0px; background-color: #ffffff; "><div class="gdlr-image-frame-item gdlr-item" style="margin-bottom: 0px;"><div class="gdlr-frame frame-type-none"><div class="gdlr-image-link-shortcode"><img src="https://res.cloudinary.com/djqswlfat/image/upload/v1776083984/vancouver-island/port-mcneill.jpg" alt="" width="1600" height="500"></div></div></div><div class="clear"></div><div class="clear"></div></div><div class="clear"></div></section></div> <!-- Sidebar With Content Section--> <div class="with-sidebar-wrapper"><section id="content-section-2"><div class="gdlr-color-wrapper gdlr-show-all no-skin" style="background-color: #efefef; "><div class="container"><div class="gdlr-item gdlr-content-item"> <p>Rich with recreation, eco-tourism possibilities and a breadth of historical cultural treasures, <strong><b>Port McNeil</b></strong>&nbsp;offers an amazing array of north-island experiences.</p> <p>Port McNeil also provides easy&nbsp;<strong><b>ferry connections </b></strong>to its neighbouring communities of <strong><b>Alert Bay</b></strong>&nbsp;and <strong><b>Sointula</b></strong>&nbsp;on nearby Cormorant and Malcolm Islands (respectively). These connections allow visitors to explore an abundance of culture within a relatively small, accessible area. Port McNeill is also the gateway to the protected <strong><b>Broughton Archipelago</b></strong>&nbsp;and sacred <strong><b>Great Bear Rainforest,</b></strong>&nbsp;world-renowned for its rugged, remote beauty and famed white Kermode or ‘Spirit Bear’.</p> <p>Port McNeill is partially named after its harbour, which is open year-round for commercial and pleasure boats of all sizes. Centrally located and close to amenities, this convenient harbour has become a major supply point for sailors travelling along the inner straits of Vancouver Island.</p> </div><div class="clear"></div><div class="clear"></div></div></div><div class="clear"></div></section><section id="content-section-3"><div class="gdlr-color-wrapper " style="background-color: #ffffff; "><div class="container"><div class="gdlr-item gdlr-content-item"><h3 style="text-align: center;">Outdoor Activities</h3> </div><div class="clear"></div><div class="gdlr-item gdlr-content-item"><h3><a href="https://www.discovervancouverisland.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/north-coast-trail.jpg"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-14116" src="https://res.cloudinary.com/djqswlfat/image/upload/v1776083985/vancouver-island/north-coast-trail.jpg" alt="" width="1200" height="900"></a></h3> <h3>Hiking</h3> <p>For a long-haul hiking adventure, head north to Cape Scott Provincial Park. The&nbsp;<a href="https://www.discovervancouverisland.com/overnight-hikes-by-region/north-island/cape-scott-trail/"><strong><u><b>Cape Scott</b></u></strong></a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a href="https://discovervancouverisland.com/overnight-hikes-by-region/north-island/the-north-coast-trail/"><strong><u><b>North Coast Trail</b></u></strong></a>&nbsp;hikes take 5-7 days each to complete.</p> <p>Day hikers can explore the <strong><b>Salt Marsh Trail</b></strong>, <strong><b>Schoolhouse Trail</b></strong>, or stroll along the <strong><b>seawall</b></strong>&nbsp;and discover the 1938 steam donkey used for logging. For more hiking opportunities, head a few minutes south to <a href="https://www.discovervancouverisland.com/regions/north-island/telegraph-cove/"><strong><u><b>Telegraph Cove</b></u></strong></a>.</p> </div><div class="clear"></div><div class="eight columns"><div class="gdlr-item gdlr-content-item"><h3><a href="https://www.discovervancouverisland.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/Nimpkish-Lake-windsurfing.jpg"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-14118" src="https://res.cloudinary.com/djqswlfat/image/upload/v1776083986/vancouver-island/nimpkish-lake-windsurfing.jpg" alt="" width="1200" height="794"></a></h3> <h3 style="text-align: left;">Windsurfing</h3> <p>Nearby <strong><b>Nimpkish Lake</b></strong>&nbsp;is a beautiful recreation area and windsurfing hot spot, thanks to strong, consistent thermal winds that blow through every summer afternoon. The highlight of the windsurfing season here is the <strong><b>Nimpkish Lake Speed Slalom Windsurfing Weekend</b></strong>, held in early August. For more information, including camping opportunities, explore our <a href="https://www.discovervancouverisland.com/windsurfing-kite-boarding/"><strong><u><b>windsurfing section</b></u></strong></a>.</p> </div></div><div class="four columns"><div class="gdlr-image-frame-item gdlr-item"><div class="gdlr-frame frame-type-solid" style="background-color: #e5e5e5;"><div class="gdlr-image-link-shortcode"><img src="https://res.cloudinary.com/djqswlfat/image/upload/v1776083986/vancouver-island/portmcnei-tml-700-1.jpg" alt="" width="700" height="463"><a href="https://www.discovervancouverisland.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/11/PortMcNei-TMl-700-1.jpg" data-rel="fancybox"><span class="gdlr-image-link-overlay">&nbsp;</span><span class="gdlr-image-link-icon"><i class="fa icon-search"></i></span></a></div></div></div></div><div class="clear"></div><div class="six columns"><div class="gdlr-item gdlr-content-item"><h3><a href="https://www.discovervancouverisland.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/broughton-archipelago.jpg"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-14119" src="https://res.cloudinary.com/djqswlfat/image/upload/v1776083806/vancouver-island/broughton-archipelago.jpg" alt="" width="1200" height="900"></a></h3> <h3>Broughton Archipelago Marine Provincial Park</h3> <p>British Columbia’s largest marine park, the <strong><b>Broughton Archipelago </b></strong>is an island chain in the <strong><b>Queen Charlotte Strait </b></strong>made up of dozens of undeveloped islands and islets. The park is teeming with wildlife, such as <strong><b>bald eagles</b></strong>, <strong><b>salmon</b></strong>, <strong><b>seals</b></strong>&nbsp;and a variety of <strong><b>whale species</b></strong>. Broughton Archipelago is best accessed by a <a href="https://www.discovervancouverisland.com/kayaking/"><u>kayak</u></a>&nbsp;or a light vessel under power. An immersive coastal experience awaits.&nbsp;</p> </div></div><div class="six columns"><div class="gdlr-item gdlr-content-item"><h3><a href="https://www.discovervancouverisland.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/robson-bight.jpg"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-14120" src="https://res.cloudinary.com/djqswlfat/image/upload/v1776083986/vancouver-island/robson-bight.jpg" alt="" width="1200" height="675"></a></h3> <h3>Robson Bight Ecological Reserve</h3> <p>The Robson Bight Ecological Reserve was established in 1982 to protect the approximately <strong><b>200 killer whales (orcas)</b></strong>&nbsp;that travel through this area annually. Motorized boats must refrain from entering the area, but <a href="https://www.discovervancouverisland.com/whale-watching-vancouver-island/"><strong><u><b>whale watching tours</b></u></strong></a>&nbsp;travel along the borders of the sanctuary to catch views of the mythical sea mammals.</p> </div></div><div class="clear"></div><div class="six columns"><div class="gdlr-item gdlr-content-item"><h3><a href="https://www.discovervancouverisland.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/robson-bight-whale.jpg"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-14122" src="https://res.cloudinary.com/djqswlfat/image/upload/v1776083986/vancouver-island/robson-bight-whale.jpg" alt="" width="1200" height="651"></a></h3> <h3>Nature &amp; Wildlife Tours</h3> <p><b></b>From <strong><b>bald eagles</b></strong>, <strong><b>to humpback whales</b></strong>&nbsp;and <strong><b>orcas</b></strong>, to <strong><b>grizzly bears</b></strong>&nbsp;and more, you are truly in wild country in this corner of Vancouver Island. With plenty of tour companies to choose from, you are sure to have the experience of a lifetime as you explore the diverse wildlife and unforgettable landscapes.&nbsp;Whether you’re a tourist hoping to see some incredible sights, or a photographer l looking to capture once-in-a-lifetime moments, there’s something for everyone in this breathtaking location.</p> </div></div><div class="six columns"><div class="gdlr-item gdlr-content-item"><h3><a href="https://www.discovervancouverisland.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/diving.jpg"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-14123" src="https://res.cloudinary.com/djqswlfat/image/upload/v1776083987/vancouver-island/diving.jpg" alt="" width="910" height="607"></a></h3> <h3>Diving</h3> <p><b></b>The northeast side of Vancouver Island is recognized as one of the best cold-water diving locations in the world. With <strong><b>crystal-clear waters</b></strong>&nbsp;and exciting sea life such as wolf eels, sea otters, ling cod, rock fish, sea anemones, and <strong><b>massive kelp beds</b></strong>, it’s an experience you won’t forget. Local dive companies can provide you with equipment for your preferred diving style and help you get the most out of your experience.</p> </div></div><div class="clear"></div><div class="six columns"><div class="gdlr-item gdlr-content-item"><h3><a href="https://www.discovervancouverisland.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/nimpkish-lake-vancouver-island.jpg"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-14124" src="https://res.cloudinary.com/djqswlfat/image/upload/v1776083987/vancouver-island/nimpkish-lake-vancouver-island.jpg" alt="" width="910" height="512"></a></h3> <h3>Nimpkish Lake / Nimpkish Lake Provincial Park</h3> <p><b></b><strong><b>Nimpkish Lake Park</b></strong>&nbsp;boasts nearly <strong><b>10,000 acres</b></strong>&nbsp;of lush west coast wilderness. Old growth hemlocks and diverse ecological and wildlife fill this protected area just south of Port McNeil. <strong><b>Nimpkish Lake</b></strong>, which is believed to be the deepest lake on Vancouver Island, offers <strong><b>canoeing</b></strong>, <strong><b>fresh water fishing</b></strong>, <strong><b>swimming</b></strong>&nbsp;and <strong><b>windsurfing</b></strong>. With the nearest lodging 30 km away in Port McNeil, Nimpkish Lake Park only offers back-country camping.</p> </div></div><div class="clear"></div></div></div><div class="clear"></div></section><section id="content-section-4"><div class="gdlr-color-wrapper gdlr-show-all no-skin" style="background-color: #efefef; "><div class="container"><div class="gdlr-item gdlr-content-item" style="margin-bottom: 100px;"><h3 style="text-align: center;"><strong><b>Visit Nearby Communities</b></strong></h3> </div><div class="clear"></div><div class="four columns"><div class="gdlr-item gdlr-content-item"><h3><a href="https://www.discovervancouverisland.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/telegraph-cove-1.jpg"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-14126" src="https://res.cloudinary.com/djqswlfat/image/upload/v1776083988/vancouver-island/telegraph-cove-1.jpg" alt="" width="1200" height="675"></a></h3> <h3>Telegraph Cove</h3> <p><strong><b><br> </b></strong><a href="https://www.discovervancouverisland.com/regions/north-island/telegraph-cove/"><u>Telegraph Cove</u></a>&nbsp;grew from a fishing and canning village into an <strong><b>eco-tourism hub</b></strong>&nbsp;attracting hundreds of thousands of visitors each year. Even though Telegraph Cove only has about 20 year-round inhabitants, this small village has much to offer. Visit the historic <strong><b>Whale Museum</b></strong>, go <strong><b>kayakin</b></strong>g, take out a <strong><b>boat and fish</b></strong>&nbsp;the stunning waters, or check out the impressive <strong><b>wildlife</b></strong>. This scenic area of the island offers incredible beauty everywhere you look, and there’s something for everyone.</p> </div></div><div class="four columns"><div class="gdlr-item gdlr-content-item"><h3><a href="https://www.discovervancouverisland.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/alert-bay-totem-poles.jpg"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-14127" src="https://res.cloudinary.com/djqswlfat/image/upload/v1776083988/vancouver-island/alert-bay-totem-poles.jpg" alt="" width="1920" height="1080"></a></h3> <h3>Alert Bay</h3> <p><strong><b>Alert Bay </b></strong>on <strong><b>Cormorant Island </b></strong>is site of the oldest community on Vancouver Island, where the ‘Namgis First Nation’s culture continues to thrive. The <strong><b>U’mista Cultural Centre </b></strong>is the longest-running First Nations museum in all of Canada. Themed <strong><b>walking tours</b></strong>&nbsp;will take you across the island, pausing to take in the <strong><b>Alert Bay Library-Museum</b></strong>, the traditional “<strong><b>Big House</b></strong>”, the <strong><b>world’s tallest totem pole</b></strong>, and more.</p> </div></div><div class="four columns"><div class="gdlr-item gdlr-content-item"><h3><a href="https://www.discovervancouverisland.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/sointula-vancouver-island.jpg"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-14128" src="https://res.cloudinary.com/djqswlfat/image/upload/v1776083988/vancouver-island/sointula-vancouver-island.jpg" alt="" width="1200" height="900"></a></h3> <h3>Sointula</h3> <p><strong><b>Sointula</b></strong>, located on <strong><b>Malcom Island</b></strong>, translates to “place of harmony” in Finnish. Like Alert Bay, Sointula also historically belonged to the ‘Namgis First Nation. This isolated village was settled in 1901 by Finnish idealists who hoped to create a utopian socialist society. While the experiment failed after a few years, the town remained and some cooperative ventures (like the <strong><b>Sointula Co-operative Store</b></strong>) still operate today. During your visit to Sointula, be sure to check out the local <strong><b>hiking trails</b></strong>, <strong><b>Sointula Museum</b></strong>, <strong><b>fishing lodge</b></strong>, and <strong><b>artist studios</b></strong>.</p> </div></div><div class="clear"></div></div></div><div class="clear"></div></section><section id="content-section-5"><div class="gdlr-color-wrapper gdlr-show-all no-skin" style="background-color: #ffffff; "><div class="container"><div class="gdlr-item gdlr-content-item"><h3 style="text-align: center;">History</h3> <p>Port McNeil and the surrounding areas have roots back as far as 9,000 years ago, with the arrival of the Kwakwaka’wakw First Nation. Today the <strong><em><b><i>‘Namgis</i></b></em></strong><strong><b>&nbsp;First Nation</b></strong>, a group within the larger Kwakwaka’wakw Nation, inhabit what is traditionally known as <strong><b>Yalis</b></strong>&nbsp;on Cormorant Island, located a short sail away from Port McNeill.</p> <p>Named after Captain William Henry McNeill of the Hudson Bay Company, Port McNeill’s foundations have been laid with an enduring pioneering spirit. Port McNeil was originally a base camp for loggers and became a British settlement in 1936. It was, notably, the first town to be incorporated under the law of the new Canadian Constitution in 1966. &nbsp;In stark contrast with its logging history, Port McNeill is now the <strong><b>reforestation capital</b></strong>&nbsp;of northern Vancouver Island.</p> </div><div class="clear"></div><div class="clear"></div></div></div><div class="clear"></div></section><section id="content-section-6"><div class="gdlr-color-wrapper gdlr-show-all no-skin" style="background-color: #efefef;
